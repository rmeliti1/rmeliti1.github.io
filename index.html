What Knot To Use?

A visual guide to common knots and their uses.

Filter by Use Case:

Bowline (Palstek)

Video by PhilEOS, CC BY-SA 3.0, via Wikimedia Commons

Description:

Creates a fixed loop at the end of a rope. Known as the "King of Knots".

Pros:

  • Strong and secure loop.
  • Relatively easy to untie, even after being loaded.
  • Doesn't slip or bind easily.

Cons:

  • Can potentially shake loose if not under load (less common with modern ropes). Consider a backup (e.g., Yosemite finish or stopper knot).
  • Cannot be tied or untied when weighted.

Typical Applications:

Rescue, mooring boats, climbing (tying in, though figure-eight is more common now), attaching rope to objects.

Square Knot (Reef Knot)

Description:

A simple knot for joining two ropes of *equal* thickness or for binding.

Pros:

  • Very simple and quick to tie.
  • Lies relatively flat.

Cons:

  • **Unsafe for critical loads.** Can capsize or slip, especially with ropes of different diameters or slippery synthetic ropes.
  • Can jam tightly, making it hard to untie after loading.

Typical Applications:

Tying bandages, securing parcels, reefing sails (its namesake), general non-critical binding. **Avoid using it to join climbing ropes or for rescue.**

Clove Hitch

Description:

Used to fasten a rope to a post, rail, or another rope.

Pros:

  • Quick and easy to tie.
  • Adjustable length without untying completely.
  • Can be tied one-handed or in the middle of a rope.

Cons:

  • Can slip under heavy or cyclical loads, especially on smooth surfaces. Often needs a backup knot (e.g., half-hitches).
  • Can bind and become difficult to untie.

Typical Applications:

Temporary moorings, starting lashings, securing tent guylines (often backed up), stagecraft rigging (non-critical), securing fenders.

Figure-Eight Knot / Follow-Through

(Shows Figure-Eight Follow-Through for loop)

(Shows simple Figure-Eight for stopper)

Description:

A fundamental knot. Used as a stopper knot on its own, or tied back on itself (follow-through) to create a strong, secure loop.

Pros:

  • Very strong and secure (especially the loop form).
  • Relatively easy to inspect for correctness.
  • Less prone to jamming than an Overhand knot.

Cons:

  • Bulkier than some other knots (e.g., Overhand stopper, Bowline loop).
  • Can be harder to untie after heavy loading than a Bowline.

Typical Applications:

Stopper: Preventing rope end from running through a device (belay device, pulley).
Loop (Follow-Through): Standard knot for tying into a climbing harness, rescue applications, securing loads.

Sheet Bend

Description:

Used for joining two ropes, especially those of *unequal* thickness. Ensure free ends are on the *same* side.

Pros:

  • Effective for joining ropes of different diameters or materials.
  • Relatively secure when properly tied and dressed.

Cons:

  • Less secure than a Figure-Eight bend or Double Fisherman's for critical loads.
  • Can work loose if not kept under tension, especially with synthetic ropes.
  • A Double Sheet Bend is more secure but bulkier.

Typical Applications:

Joining flags to halyards, extending ropes, net making, general utility where ropes of different sizes need connecting (non-critical).

Taut-Line Hitch

(Midshipman's Hitch shown - often preferred, works similarly)

Description:

Creates an adjustable loop or attaches a rope to an object, allowing the line to be tightened or loosened.

Pros:

  • Adjustable under tension.
  • Grips well when loaded.
  • Relatively easy to tie.

Cons:

  • Can slip if not tied correctly or with very slick ropes.
  • Less secure than fixed loops for permanent or critical attachments.

Typical Applications:

Tent guylines, clotheslines, securing loads on trailers or roof racks (where adjustability is needed), securing tarps.

Two Half-Hitches

Description:

A very simple and common hitch for temporarily securing a rope to a post, ring, or rail.

Pros:

  • Extremely easy and quick to tie.
  • Easy to remember.

Cons:

  • Not very secure; can slip or bind, especially under heavy or variable load.
  • Not suitable for critical applications or smooth objects.
  • Less secure than a Clove Hitch or Bowline.

Typical Applications:

Temporary fastening of light loads, tying painters to docks (briefly), clotheslines, general non-critical utility hitching.

Overhand Knot

Description:

The simplest knot, forms the basis for many others. Used primarily as a basic stopper knot.

Pros:

  • Extremely simple and fast to tie.
  • Small and compact.

Cons:

  • Can jam very tightly, becoming extremely difficult or impossible to untie after heavy loading.
  • Weakens the rope more than other stopper knots like the Figure-Eight.
  • Can roll off the end of the rope under certain conditions.

Typical Applications:

Simple stopper knot for non-critical uses (e.g., preventing thread end from fraying), temporary knot at rope end, base for other knots (like the Water Knot).

Alpine Butterfly Loop

Description:

Forms a secure, fixed loop in the middle of a rope, without needing access to the ends.

Pros:

  • Strong and stable loop that can be loaded in any direction (loop itself, or either standing end).
  • Relatively easy to untie, even after loading.
  • Can be tied in the bight (middle) of the rope.

Cons:

  • Slightly more complex to tie than some other loops.
  • Can be visually confusing to inspect if unfamiliar.

Typical Applications:

Climbing (isolating damaged rope section, creating mid-rope anchor points, glacier travel), rescue work, creating attachment points along a line.

Double Fisherman's Bend

Description:

A very strong and secure method for joining two ropes, especially similar-diameter synthetic ropes.

Pros:

  • Extremely secure and reliable, standard for joining climbing ropes.
  • Relatively compact.

Cons:

  • Can become virtually impossible to untie after being heavily loaded, often considered permanent.
  • Can be difficult to tie correctly and requires careful dressing.

Typical Applications:

Joining climbing ropes (e.g., for rappelling), making rope loops (prussik loops, cordelettes), any application requiring a highly secure, near-permanent join.

Prusik Knot

Description:

A friction hitch tied with a loop of cord around a thicker rope. Grips when loaded, slides when unloaded.

Pros:

  • Grips well in both directions along the main rope.
  • Can be slid along the rope when not weighted.
  • Relatively simple concept.

Cons:

  • Requires a loop of cord (usually thinner than the main rope).
  • Can jam after heavy loading or slip if the diameter difference isn't sufficient or if ropes are wet/icy.
  • Other friction hitches (e.g., Klemheist, Bachmann) may perform better in some situations.

Typical Applications:

Climbing (ascending ropes, progress capture in hauling systems, rappel backup - use caution), rescue work, arborist work.

Trucker's Hitch

(Shows one common version)

Description:

A compound knot system providing mechanical advantage (typically 3:1) to tighten a line securely.

Pros:

  • Provides significant tightening power.
  • Relatively easy to release.
  • Versatile for securing loads.

Cons:

  • More complex than simple hitches.
  • Reliability depends on the loop knot used (e.g., slippery loop can fail).
  • Can cause high friction and wear on the rope at the loop turn.

Typical Applications:

Securing loads on trucks, trailers, roof racks; creating high-tension ridgelines for tarps; clotheslines; general load securing.
