Bowline (Palstek)

Video by PhilEOS, CC BY-SA 3.0, via Wikimedia Commons

Description:

Creates a reliable fixed loop at the end of a rope. Known as the "King of Knots".

Strength Impact:
Significant reduction (~40-50%).
Ease of Untying:
Relatively easy, even after heavy load.
Security Note:
Can shake loose if unloaded; consider backup/stopper knot for critical applications.

Pros:

  • Strong and secure loop (when loaded).
  • Doesn't slip or bind easily.

Cons:

  • Can potentially shake loose if not under load.
  • Cannot be tied/untied when weighted.
  • Lower breaking strength compared to Figure Eight loop.

Typical Applications:

Rescue, mooring boats, attaching rope to objects, general utility loop.

Square Knot (Reef Knot)

Description:

A simple knot for joining two ropes of *equal* thickness or for binding.

Strength Impact:
Significant reduction (~50-60%). Unreliable.
Ease of Untying:
Can jam badly after moderate loading.
Security Note:
**Unsafe for critical loads.** Prone to capsizing or slipping, especially with synthetic ropes.

Pros:

  • Very simple and quick to tie.
  • Lies relatively flat.

Cons:

  • Unreliable and unsafe for critical loads.
  • Can capsize or slip easily.
  • Jams tightly.

Typical Applications:

Tying bandages, securing parcels (non-critical), reefing sails (its namesake). **Avoid for safety applications.**

Clove Hitch

Description:

Fastens a rope to a post, rail, or carabiner.

Strength Impact:
Moderate reduction (~30-40%).
Ease of Untying:
Can bind, moderately difficult after heavy load.
Security Note:
Can slip under cyclical loads or vibrations; often requires backup (e.g., half-hitch).

Pros:

  • Quick and easy to tie, even one-handed.
  • Adjustable.
  • Can be tied mid-rope or at the end.

Cons:

  • Can slip, especially on smooth surfaces or with vibrations.
  • Can bind.

Typical Applications:

Temporary moorings, starting lashings, tent guylines (with backup), attaching to carabiners (climbing anchors - adjustable).

Figure-Eight Knot / Follow-Through

(Figure-Eight Follow-Through for loop)

(Simple Figure-Eight for stopper)

Description:

Fundamental knot used as a stopper or tied back on itself (follow-through) for a strong loop.

Strength Impact:
Low reduction (Loop: ~20-25%). Strongest common end loop.
Ease of Untying:
Moderately difficult after very heavy load, but better than Overhand.
Security Note:
Very secure and reliable loop. Easy to visually inspect for correctness.

Pros:

  • Very strong and secure loop.
  • Relatively easy to inspect.
  • Good stopper knot (less prone to jamming than Overhand).

Cons:

  • Bulkier than some alternatives (Bowline, Overhand).
  • Can be harder to untie after extreme loading than a Bowline.

Typical Applications:

Stopper: Preventing rope end passing through device.
Loop: Standard for climbing harnesses, rescue, critical load attachment.

Sheet Bend

Description:

Joins two ropes, especially of unequal thickness. Working ends must be on the *same* side.

Strength Impact:
Moderate reduction (~40-50%).
Ease of Untying:
Moderate difficulty.
Security Note:
Less secure than Figure Eight Bend or Double Fisherman's. Can work loose if not under tension. Double Sheet Bend is more secure.

Pros:

  • Effective for joining ropes of different diameters/materials.
  • Relatively simple and quick.

Cons:

  • Not for critical loads.
  • Can slip if not kept taut.
  • Less secure than other bends.

Typical Applications:

Joining flags to halyards, extending ropes temporarily, net making, general utility (non-critical).

Taut-Line Hitch

Description:

Creates an adjustable loop or attaches to an object, allowing the line to be tightened. (Midshipman's Hitch often preferred).

Strength Impact:
Moderate reduction (depends on friction).
Ease of Untying:
Generally easy.
Security Note:
Can slip on very slick ropes or if not dressed properly. Test under load.

Pros:

  • Adjustable under tension.
  • Grips well when loaded (usually).

Cons:

  • Can slip if tied incorrectly or with very slick ropes.
  • Less secure than fixed loops for critical loads.

Typical Applications:

Tent guylines, clotheslines, securing tarps, adjustable load securing (non-critical).

Two Half-Hitches

Description:

Very simple hitch for temporarily securing a rope to a post or ring.

Strength Impact:
Significant reduction (~50%+). Unreliable.
Ease of Untying:
Can jam, moderate difficulty.
Security Note:
Not secure for important loads. Can slip easily. Often needs additional hitches or backup.

Pros:

  • Extremely easy and quick to tie.
  • Easy to remember.

Cons:

  • Not very secure; slips easily.
  • Can bind.
  • Unsuitable for critical applications.

Typical Applications:

Temporary fastening of light loads, brief mooring (very light boat), clotheslines (may slip).

Overhand Knot

Description:

The simplest knot, basis for many others. Used primarily as a basic stopper.

Strength Impact:
Significant reduction (~50%+).
Ease of Untying:
Jams very badly after loading; often impossible to untie.
Security Note:
Weakest stopper knot. Can roll off the end under load. Avoid for critical uses.

Pros:

  • Extremely simple and fast.
  • Small and compact.
  • Foundation for other knots.

Cons:

  • Jams very tightly.
  • Weakens the rope significantly.
  • Can roll/fail under load.

Typical Applications:

Simple non-critical stopper, temporary knot, base for other knots (e.g., Water Knot, Fisherman's Knot).

Alpine Butterfly Loop

Description:

Forms a secure, stable loop in the middle of a rope.

Strength Impact:
Moderate reduction (~35-45%).
Ease of Untying:
Relatively easy, even after loading.
Security Note:
Very reliable when tied correctly. Loadable in 3 directions.

Pros:

  • Strong and stable loop, loadable in three directions.
  • Easy to untie after loading.
  • Can be tied in the bight (mid-rope).

Cons:

  • Slightly more complex to learn than Bowline/Figure Eight.
  • Can be visually confusing to inspect if unfamiliar.

Typical Applications:

Climbing (isolating rope damage, mid-rope anchors, glacier travel), rescue, creating fixed loops along a line.

Double Fisherman's Bend

Description:

Very strong and secure method for joining two ropes, especially similar-diameter synthetic ropes.

Strength Impact:
Low reduction (~20-30%). One of the strongest bends.
Ease of Untying:
Extremely difficult/impossible after loading (considered permanent).
Security Note:
Very reliable for critical joins. Ensure sufficient tail length.

Pros:

  • Extremely secure and reliable bend.
  • Relatively compact.
  • Standard for joining climbing ropes.

Cons:

  • Considered permanent after heavy loading.
  • Can be difficult to tie correctly; requires careful dressing.

Typical Applications:

Joining climbing ropes (rappelling), making cordelettes/prusik loops, critical/permanent rope joining.

Prusik Knot

Description:

A friction hitch tied with a loop of cord around a thicker rope. Grips when loaded, slides when unloaded.

Strength Impact:
Does not weaken the main rope; relies on strength of cord loop.
Ease of Untying:
Usually easy, can jam slightly after very heavy load.
Security Note:
Requires correct cord diameter ratio (typically ~60-80% of main rope). Can slip on wet/icy ropes. Test function before critical use.

Pros:

  • Grips well in both directions along the main rope.
  • Can be slid along the rope when not weighted.
  • Relatively simple concept.

Cons:

  • Requires a pre-tied loop of appropriate cord.
  • Can jam after heavy load cycles.
  • May slip on wet/icy ropes. Other friction hitches may perform better.

Typical Applications:

Climbing (ascending ropes, progress capture, rappel backup - use caution), rescue, arborist work.

Trucker's Hitch

Description:

Compound knot system providing mechanical advantage (typically 3:1) to tighten a line securely.

Strength Impact:
Variable; limited by the strength of the loop knot used and friction point.
Ease of Untying:
Relatively easy to release tension; loop knot might jam.
Security Note:
Reliability depends heavily on the loop knot used (e.g., avoid Slippery Half Hitch for loop). Rope can wear at friction point.

Pros:

  • Provides significant tightening power (mechanical advantage).
  • Relatively easy to release tension.
  • Versatile for securing loads.

Cons:

  • More complex than simple hitches.
  • Strength/reliability depends on loop choice.
  • Can cause high friction and rope wear.

Typical Applications:

Securing loads on trucks/trailers/racks, creating high-tension ridgelines, clotheslines, general load securing.

Rolling Hitch

Description:

A friction hitch used to attach a rope to another rope, pole, or cable, especially for pulling along the object.

Strength Impact:
Moderate reduction.
Ease of Untying:
Generally easy.
Security Note:
Grips securely when pulled in line with the standing part (direction of the turns). May slip if pulled sideways or on very slick surfaces.

Pros:

  • Grips well when pulled in one direction along the object.
  • Can be slid along the object when slack.
  • Relatively easy to tie.

Cons:

  • Less secure if the pull is not aligned correctly.
  • May slip on very smooth or tapered objects.

Typical Applications:

Creating adjustable snubbers on mooring lines, attaching a rope to a taut line (e.g., anchor rode), hauling along a fixed line, backup for other hitches.
